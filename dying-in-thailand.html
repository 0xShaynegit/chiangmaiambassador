Practical Expat Guidance

When a foreigner dies in Thailand. What you need to know before you need to know it.

Nobody moves to Chiang Mai thinking about this. But it happens. I have helped families navigate it more times than I would like. This is the guide I wish everyone read before the moment arrived.

Category Home / Expat Life / Essential Guides
Read time 10 min
Last updated June 2026

TL;DR: Key Facts

  • Thailand requires a death certificate (ใบมรณะบัตร) issued by the local district office (Amphoe) before anything else can happen.
  • Your embassy must be notified. They issue an official death notification, not a Thai document.
  • Repatriation of remains is expensive: budget 150,000 to 400,000+ THB (approx. $6,000 to $16,000+ AUD) depending on destination and circumstances.
  • Local Buddhist cremation at a temple (Wat) is a respectful, lower-cost alternative: typically 5,000 to 30,000 THB.
  • Without a Thai will or Power of Attorney, accessing a deceased person's assets in Thailand can take months or years.
  • Travel insurance often covers repatriation. Standard health insurance usually does not. Check now, not later.

Over sixteen years in Chiang Mai, I have sat with families who had no idea what to do. I have helped coordinate the aftermath when friends passed without warning. I have seen people navigate the system well and I have seen people make it much harder than it needed to be.

Most of them had never thought about it. Why would you? You come to Chiang Mai to live. The food is good. The cost is manageable. The community is real. Death is not part of the plan.

But Thailand is home for many of us now. And home is where life happens, including the end of it. This guide exists because several people I knew passed with varying levels of preparation, and the difference in how those situations unfolded was significant. Not talking about this is not protecting yourself. It is just leaving the people who love you with more to carry.

Read this once. Do two or three things. Then put it away and get back to living.

Step One: The Death Certificate

In Thailand, the official death certificate is called a ใบมรณะบัตร (Bai Marana Bat). It is issued by the local district office, the Amphoe (อำเภอ). In Chiang Mai, this is typically Amphoe Mueang.

How you get there depends on how the death occurred.

Death in hospital

The hospital issues an initial death notification document. This is not the legal death certificate. Take that document to the Amphoe within 24 hours to register the death and receive the official certificate. The hospital will usually guide you through this, especially the larger private hospitals like Bangkok Hospital Chiang Mai (Soi 7, Nimmanhaemin Road) and Chiang Mai Ram Hospital (on Boonruangrit Road). They have experience with foreign nationals.

Death at home or in unexplained circumstances

Call the police first. A police report is required before the death can be registered. Do not move the body. The Tourist Police number in Chiang Mai is 1155. For emergencies, dial 191. The process takes longer but the steps are the same: police report, then Amphoe, then certificate.

What you need at the Amphoe

  • The deceased's passport
  • Hospital death notification or police report
  • A translator if you do not have Thai (Amphoe staff will have limited English)
  • Your own ID

The Amphoe will issue the death certificate in Thai. You will need a certified translation for your home country's purposes. Keep multiple certified copies. You will need more than you think.

Step Two: Contact the Embassy

The embassy of the deceased's home country needs to be notified as soon as possible. This is not optional and it is not just a formality. The embassy issues a formal death notification for the home country's records and is the starting point for repatriation paperwork.

The nearest consular offices for most nationalities are in Chiang Mai or Bangkok. For a full list of embassies and consulates, see our Embassies and Consulates guide.

What embassies can do:

  • Issue an official notification of death for home country records
  • Provide a list of local funeral homes experienced with international repatriation
  • Contact next of kin on your behalf if needed
  • Assist with emergency travel documents if family need to travel urgently

What embassies cannot do: pay for anything, make decisions on your behalf, or speed up Thai bureaucracy.

The Repatriation Question

This is where most families face their hardest decision, and where the costs vary most dramatically.

Repatriation means sending the body home. It requires embalming, a specific airline-approved shipping coffin, health certificates, Thai government clearance, and the airline's own approval process. The logistics are handled by specialist funeral homes. In Chiang Mai, there are a handful with experience in international cases.

Item Estimated Cost (THB) Approx. AUD
Embalming and preparation 15,000 - 40,000 $600 - $1,600
International shipping coffin 20,000 - 60,000 $800 - $2,400
Documentation and permits 5,000 - 15,000 $200 - $600
Air freight (to Australia/UK/USA) 80,000 - 250,000+ $3,200 - $10,000+
Funeral home service fee 20,000 - 50,000 $800 - $2,000
Total estimate 140,000 - 415,000+ $5,600 - $16,600+

These are 2026 estimates. The destination country, the size of the person, the airline available from Chiang Mai at the time, and whether you need refrigerated storage while documents are processed all affect the final cost. Long weekends and public holidays add days to the process.

Payment is required upfront. Funeral homes will not begin work without it.

Local Cremation: A Respectful Alternative

Many long-term expats have made their wishes clear: do not send me home. Cremate me here.

Thai Buddhist cremation at a Wat (temple) is a genuinely dignified process. The ceremonies typically run for three to seven days, with monks chanting each morning and evening. The community gathers. There is food. There is meaning. It is nothing like the clinical efficiency of a Western funeral home.

Costs for local cremation are a fraction of repatriation:

  • Basic temple cremation: 5,000 to 15,000 THB ($200 to $600 AUD)
  • With ceremony and monk services: 15,000 to 40,000 THB ($600 to $1,600 AUD)
  • Premium facility with Western-style options: 40,000 to 80,000 THB

Ashes can then be divided: some scattered here, some sent home. Airlines accept cremated remains as checked luggage with the right documentation. This is often the most practical outcome for families who want something at home and something here.

If the deceased had Thai friends and a life here, attending a Thai Buddhist cremation ceremony is one of the most moving things you will ever experience. It is worth understanding what you are part of if it happens.

Wills, Assets, and the Legal Mess Nobody Warns You About

Thailand recognises foreign wills, but enforcing one here involves the Thai court system. It is slow. It is expensive. It is done in Thai.

If you have assets in Thailand, a Thai will is strongly advised. A Thai will (พินัยกรรม) can be handwritten, typed, or registered at the Amphoe or a Land Office. A lawyer costs 3,000 to 10,000 THB to draft a simple one. That is a fraction of what the court process costs if you die without one.

For expats who want specialist help with wills and estate planning, Ken Brown Financial Consultants offers dedicated wills and estate planning for expats. They understand the specific complications of cross-border estates, Thai legal requirements, and how assets held in multiple countries interact with each other. If you have property, superannuation, investments, or a pension back home alongside assets here, this is not something to do with a general Thai lawyer who does not understand international estate law. The cost of getting it right is small. The cost of getting it wrong falls on the people you leave behind.

Having both a Thai will for Thailand-based assets and a current home-country will for overseas assets is the correct approach for most long-term expats. Ken Brown's team works across both jurisdictions and can advise on which assets need to be addressed where. If you have not reviewed your estate documents since moving to Thailand, that is the first thing to fix.

Common assets that become problems:

  • Bank accounts: Thai banks freeze accounts on notification of death. Accessing funds requires a court order for foreign nationals without a Thai beneficiary on the account.
  • Condo ownership: Foreign-owned condos (under the 49% quota) can be sold or transferred, but it requires legal process and takes time.
  • Long-term leases: These do not automatically transfer. Check your lease agreement now.
  • Vehicles: Registered in your name means paperwork for whoever inherits.

Power of Attorney

A Power of Attorney (POA) allows a trusted person in Thailand to act on your behalf while you are alive but incapacitated, or to manage immediate affairs after death before formal legal process begins. Have one. It does not need to be complex. It needs to exist.

Insurance: The Gap Most People Discover Too Late

This is where I have seen the most painful surprises.

Standard health insurance in Thailand typically covers medical treatment. It does not cover the cost of repatriation. Read your policy. Look for the words "repatriation of mortal remains" or "repatriation of body." If they are not there, that benefit is not there.

Travel insurance often includes repatriation cover. If you hold an annual travel policy, check it. Some long-stay travellers use annual travel policies specifically for this reason.

Dedicated expat health insurance policies from providers like AXA, BUPA, or Cigna typically include repatriation as standard. If you are on a basic local Thai health plan, verify this gap now.

Some nationalities have government-assisted repatriation schemes for citizens who die abroad. Check with your embassy what yours offers.

What to Do If a Friend Dies: The First 24 Hours

Immediate Steps (First 24 Hours)

  • If at home or unexplained: call police (191 or Tourist Police 1155) before moving anything
  • If in hospital: ask for the hospital's international liaison or social worker
  • Locate the deceased's passport. Keep it safe. Everything depends on it.
  • Contact their home country embassy or consulate
  • Find their travel or health insurance documents. Call the insurer's emergency line immediately.
  • Identify next of kin. Contact them before social media knows anything.
  • Do not authorise or pay for anything until you know what insurance covers
  • Register the death at the Amphoe with the hospital document or police report

Thailand's heat means preservation is urgent. Hospitals hold bodies in refrigeration. Funeral homes do the same. But do not delay on the paperwork because the clock is running on storage costs as well.

The community here is genuinely good at this. If you post in the right Chiang Mai Facebook groups, people who have been through it will show up. That is what this community does.

What to Have Ready While You Are Still Here

This is the part most people skip. It takes less than an afternoon.

Your Preparedness Checklist

  • Write down your wishes: local cremation or repatriation. Tell someone.
  • Keep a copy of your passport somewhere accessible to a trusted person
  • Note your insurance policy numbers and emergency contact lines in one document
  • Name a next of kin contact, with their phone number, somewhere findable
  • If you have Thai assets: speak to a Thai lawyer about a simple will
  • If you have Thai bank accounts: add a beneficiary or trusted signatory where possible
  • Consider a Power of Attorney for a trusted person here
  • Check your health or travel insurance for repatriation cover. Right now.
  • Tell one person where everything is

Understanding the Thai Buddhist Context

If you are supporting a Thai family who has lost someone, or attending ceremonies for a friend whose family chose a Buddhist cremation, a few things help.

The body is dressed and placed in a coffin at the temple. Monks lead chanting sessions, typically morning and evening, for a period of three to seven days depending on the family's means and wishes. Guests bring money (in an envelope, not a card) and food. Dress modestly in dark or muted colours. White is acceptable, red and bright colours are not.

The cremation itself happens at the end of this period in a specific structure at the temple called a meru. It is not somber in the Western sense. There are moments of laughter, community, and genuine warmth. The Thai concept of death sits differently to many Western traditions. Being present, respectful, and following the lead of those around you is enough.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a foreigner be buried in Thailand?

Yes. Christian cemeteries in Chiang Mai accept foreign nationals. The Old Christian Cemetery on Chang Moi Road is one option. A local funeral director can advise on current availability and costs. Burial is less common than cremation here due to climate and cost.

How long does the repatriation process take?

In straightforward cases with insurance in place: five to ten days. In cases involving police investigation, no insurance, or complex documentation: several weeks. Public holidays add time. Do not expect it to be fast.

What if the person died without a will and has money in a Thai bank?

You will need to apply for a court order through the Thai legal system to access the funds. This can take six months to two years and will require a Thai lawyer. This is why having a simple will, or adding a trusted beneficiary to accounts, matters.

Does the Thai government notify the home country automatically?

No. The notification responsibility sits with whoever is managing the situation on the ground. The embassy needs to be called directly.

What happens if no one in Thailand knows the person who died?

The embassy takes on a more active role. Thai authorities will attempt to identify next of kin through the passport. If no one can be reached, the body will be held for a defined period before the state handles disposal. This is a situation that happens more often than people realise, particularly with older solo travellers.

Can ashes be taken on a plane back home?

Yes. Cremated remains are permitted as checked luggage or carry-on on most airlines, with documentation. You will need the Thai cremation certificate and a translation. Check with the airline directly as rules vary.

Guru Tip

  1. Create a one-page document today. One page. Your passport number, your insurance policy and emergency number, your next of kin name and phone, your wishes (here or home), and the name of one trusted person in Chiang Mai who has a copy. Store it in your email drafts, your Notes app, and give a physical copy to someone you trust. This document takes 20 minutes to write and removes an enormous burden from the people who would otherwise be scrambling to find answers in a foreign country while grieving. I have seen what the absence of this piece of paper does to people. Do not leave it for later.

A Final Word

I wrote this because I have sat with enough families in hard moments to know that the ones who had thought about it even a little were the ones who got through it with more grace. Not because they were more prepared for the grief. No one is. But because the practical chaos was smaller. Because one phone call led somewhere. Because a piece of paper existed.

Chiang Mai is a place where people build real lives. Real lives deserve a little planning for the end of them. That is not morbid. It is just honest.
